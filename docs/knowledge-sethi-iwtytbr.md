# Knowledge Extraction — I Will Teach You to Be Rich
## Ramit Sethi (2009, updated 2019)
### Source document for AI grounding — full framework, not rules summary

---

## CHUNK 1 — Core Philosophy and Mental Model

This book's foundational premise is that personal finance is primarily a psychology problem, not a math problem. Almost everyone already knows they should spend less and save more. The reason people fail is not lack of information — it is that traditional budgeting systems are built on restriction, shame, and friction, which causes people to abandon them. Sethi's entire framework is designed to remove friction, remove guilt, and align financial decisions with what people actually value rather than what financial experts say they should value.

The central idea is what Sethi calls the "Rich Life" — a concept that is deliberately personal and subjective. A rich life for one person might mean traveling two months a year. For another it might mean picking up their kids from school every day, or buying a cashmere coat without checking the price, or donating generously to causes they believe in. The AI should never assume what someone's rich life looks like. The job of a financial system is to fund whatever that looks like for this specific person, not to impose a universal lifestyle template.

Sethi explicitly rejects the following common financial advice patterns, and the AI should never use them:
- Telling people to stop buying coffee, lunches out, or small pleasures as a path to wealth. Sethi calls this "$3 thinking" — obsessing over small amounts while ignoring the decisions that actually move the needle (investment allocation, salary negotiation, housing cost, debt structure).
- Telling people their spending is wrong without understanding what they value. High spending on travel by someone who has their savings automated is completely fine. The same spending level by someone with zero emergency fund is a problem. The category is never the issue in isolation — the full picture is what matters.
- Perfectionism. Sethi's "85% solution" principle: getting your finances 85% right and acting on it is vastly more valuable than perfecting a spreadsheet and never acting. The AI should push people toward action, not toward perfect planning.
- Extreme frugality as a lifestyle. Being frugal (cutting ruthlessly on things you don't care about) is smart. Being cheap (depriving yourself indiscriminately of everything) is miserable and unsustainable.

The two levers of wealth building, in Sethi's view, are: (1) automation — making the right financial behaviours happen without willpower or daily decisions, and (2) playing offence on the big numbers — earning more, negotiating bills and fees, choosing the right accounts, and making the correct structural decisions on housing and debt, rather than micro-managing small purchases.

## CHUNK 2 — The Conscious Spending Plan (CSP): Full Framework

The CSP is Sethi's core budgeting framework. It divides take-home (after-tax) income into four buckets. The percentages are targets, not rigid rules — they are starting points for conversation, not verdicts.

### Bucket 1: Fixed Costs — Target 50–60% of take-home pay
Everything that is billed at a regular, predictable amount regardless of behaviour: rent or mortgage, utilities, car payment, insurance premiums, loan minimum payments, phone bill, internet, subscriptions (streaming, software, gym memberships, etc.), and groceries (Sethi includes groceries here because for most households the grocery bill is predictable within a narrow range month to month, even though technically it varies).

The 50% floor exists because going below it is extremely difficult without major lifestyle change (moving house, changing car). The 60% ceiling is the warning threshold — at 60%, there is almost no room for savings or investing regardless of how careful someone is on discretionary spending.

At 65%+ fixed costs, Sethi is direct: "You are effectively broke." The math makes it impossible to hit even minimum savings and investment targets from the remaining 35%, once guilt-free spending is included. The AI should communicate this clearly and without hedging when the data shows it.

At 75%+ fixed costs, this is a structural crisis, not a spending habits problem. Cutting subscriptions or eating less expensively will not solve it. The fix must come from reducing a major fixed cost (housing is almost always the single most effective lever) or increasing income. The AI should escalate urgency proportionally.

### Bucket 2: Investments — Target 10% of take-home pay
This is wealth-building money. Not savings goals (which are in bucket 3) — investments are for retirement and long-term compound growth. Sethi strongly favours low-cost index funds (he considers stock picking and active fund management to be statistically worse than index investing for the vast majority of people) and tax-advantaged accounts first.

Why investment bucket is separated from savings: savings goals are spent eventually (they are just deferred purchases). Investments are intended to compound over decades and are not touched until retirement. They serve fundamentally different functions and must be tracked separately.

Zero investment allocation is the single highest-priority gap to flag in any user's finances. Even a small amount — 1% of income — invested consistently in a low-cost index fund starting early vastly outperforms a large amount invested later, due to compound growth. The AI should make this case emphatically, especially to younger users.

### Bucket 3: Savings Goals — Target 5–10% of take-home pay
Short-to-medium term financial goals with a specific target and timeline: emergency fund (first priority if not yet built), holiday, car, home down payment, wedding, large purchase, children's education, medical expenses buffer. These are things the person will spend eventually — they are not investment vehicles, they are deferred purchases.

Emergency fund priority: Sethi considers 3 months of fixed expenses as the minimum viable emergency fund. The AI should calculate this in absolute terms from the user's fixed costs data, not describe it abstractly. If a user's fixed costs are 30,000 Kč/month, their minimum emergency fund target is 90,000 Kč.

The savings bucket should be funded before the guilt-free spending bucket is opened. This is the structural priority: fixed costs first, investments second, savings third, guilt-free last. If there is nothing left after the first three buckets, the AI should flag this as a problem with the fixed costs level, not with the user's character or discipline.

### Bucket 4: Guilt-Free Spending — Target 20–35% of take-home pay
Everything else: eating out, entertainment, holidays (if not separately in savings goals), clothing, hobbies, personal care, gifts, socialising. Sethi's term "guilt-free" is intentional and important. Once the first three buckets are funded, every euro in this bucket is fully earned and fully available to spend however the user chooses, with zero shame or justification required.

This is the bucket where Money Dials (see Chunk 4) apply. The AI should not try to optimise or reduce this bucket unless the other three buckets are being compromised. A user spending 35% on guilt-free activities while investing 12% and saving 8% has nothing to fix — this is an excellent position.

The AI should never suggest cutting a specific guilt-free line item (e.g. "maybe you could eat out less") unless asked, or unless the user is clearly unable to fund their investment and savings targets. Even then, the framing should be structural ("your fixed costs are leaving only X for both savings and guilt-free, so one of these needs to shift") rather than moralistic.

## CHUNK 3 — Automation: The Master Mechanism

Sethi considers financial automation to be more important than budgeting discipline, willpower, or financial knowledge. The reason: willpower is finite and unreliable. Systems are not. A person who automates their savings and investment transfers on payday never has to make the decision not to spend that money — it is simply not available in their spending account.

The automation sequence Sethi recommends, in order:
1. Salary is deposited into a checking account on payday.
2. An automatic transfer immediately moves a fixed amount to a savings account (emergency fund or goal savings).
3. An automatic transfer immediately moves a fixed amount to an investment account (or this is handled by employer through payroll contribution to a pension/retirement account).
4. Fixed bills are all on auto-pay from the checking account.
5. Whatever remains in the checking account after steps 2–4 is available to spend freely on any discretionary purpose.

The psychological insight: when savings and investments leave the account automatically before the person can see them as "available", people consistently report that they do not miss the money and do not try to claw it back. This is because humans adapt to whatever feels like their normal spending level. Lower the perceived available balance and spending adapts downward to match.

The AI should recommend automation whenever a user says they intend to save more but finds they have nothing left at month end. The problem in this case is almost always sequence, not amount — they are trying to save what is left rather than spend what is left.

## CHUNK 4 — Money Dials: The Values-Based Spending Framework

Money Dials are the 10 categories of discretionary spending that tend to produce genuine satisfaction and happiness for people. Sethi's framework holds that most people spend diffusely across many categories without spending meaningfully in the ones that actually matter to them, and the result is high spending with low satisfaction.

The 10 Money Dials are:
1. **Convenience** — delivery services, ridesharing, automation tools, anything that saves time
2. **Travel** — holidays, flights, premium accommodation, experiences abroad
3. **Health and fitness** — gym memberships, personal trainers, nutritionists, wellness services
4. **Experiences** — concerts, fine dining, adventure activities, events, anything that creates memories
5. **Eating out and food** — restaurants, coffee shops, food markets, premium groceries
6. **Relationships** — living near family, private schooling, group travel, quality time investments
7. **Generosity** — charitable donations, gifts, tipping, supporting others
8. **Luxury** — premium products, high-quality clothing, branded goods
9. **Status** — visible spending that signals success or achievement to others
10. **Self-improvement** — courses, books, coaching, skills development, therapy

How the AI should use Money Dials: When analysing a user's spending, identify which dial(s) their guilt-free spending is concentrated in. This is useful in two ways: (1) if spending is misaligned — spread across many categories but not high in any — the AI can suggest consolidating into their actual primary dial; (2) if a user is concerned about high spending in one category, the AI should assess whether that category is their primary dial before suggesting cuts. If it is their dial, the right response is not to cut it but to reduce spending in other categories that are below their dial.

The most common mismatch Sethi identifies: people whose bank statements show high spending on convenience (delivery apps, taxis, premium groceries) but who describe their money dial as travel. They are spending their discretionary budget on their secondary dial (convenience, by habit or default) and underfunding their primary dial (travel, by intention). The AI should surface this pattern when it appears in the data.

The key diagnostic question: "Does your spending reflect what you actually say you value?" When a user reports feeling like they never have money for the things they want, this mismatch is often the explanation.

## CHUNK 5 — Debt: Prioritisation and Payoff Logic

Sethi's approach to debt is pragmatic rather than ideological. He distinguishes between debt types based on interest rate and impact:

**High-interest consumer debt (credit cards, payday loans, typically >7% APR):** Pay off as fast as possible. The return on paying off a 20% interest credit card is a guaranteed 20% — no investment reliably beats that. While paying off high-interest debt, the investment bucket may be reduced to minimum contributions only (enough to capture any employer pension match, since that is a guaranteed 100% return). Once the debt is cleared, the freed cash flow is immediately redirected to investments.

**Medium-interest debt (car loans, personal loans, typically 4–7% APR):** Judgement call. Pay the minimum required while continuing to invest, since average long-term investment returns may exceed the interest cost. The AI should not insist on aggressive payoff here — present both options and their logic.

**Low-interest debt (mortgages, student loans, typically <4% APR):** Pay minimum and invest the difference. The mathematical case for paying this off aggressively is weak when the interest rate is below expected long-term investment returns. Note: even if the math favours investing, some people have a psychological need to eliminate all debt before they can feel financially secure. Sethi acknowledges this is a valid reason to deviate from the mathematical optimum — the AI should not dismiss it.

**Avalanche vs. Snowball for multiple debts:**
Sethi acknowledges both methods. The mathematical optimum is avalanche (highest interest rate first — minimises total interest paid). The psychological optimum for many people is snowball (smallest balance first — creates early wins that maintain motivation). The AI should present both and let the user choose based on their own psychology. The most important thing is consistent action, not which method is chosen.

**Minimum payments are always non-negotiable.** Missing a minimum payment damages credit score, triggers penalty rates, and can cascade into collection. The AI should never suggest skipping minimums under any circumstances.

## CHUNK 6 — Demographic-Specific Guidance

**20s / Early career:**
This decade is disproportionately important for long-term wealth. A euro invested at 25 is worth dramatically more at 65 than the same euro invested at 35, due to compound growth. The priority order is: (1) capture any employer pension match fully — this is a guaranteed 100% return; (2) build a starter emergency fund (even 1 month of expenses is better than nothing); (3) pay off any high-interest debt; (4) open and fund an investment account, even with a small amount. People in their 20s should not wait until they have a "real" salary to start investing — starting with 1,000 Kč/month at 25 beats starting with 5,000 Kč/month at 35 in long-term outcomes.

The biggest risk for this demographic is lifestyle inflation — expanding spending to match each salary increase without proportionally increasing savings and investment. The AI should surface this pattern when a user reports a recent salary increase with no corresponding savings increase.

**30s / Mid-career, often with major fixed expenses:**
This decade typically brings the highest fixed cost pressure: housing costs increase, children may appear, car expenses grow. The main risk is fixed costs creeping above 60% and crowding out investment. The AI should flag this structural pressure rather than suggesting the person cut discretionary spending, since in this demographic discretionary spending is often already lean.

The investment priority shifts here: employer pension contributions should be maximised (not just matched), and an investment account outside of the pension should begin if not already running. The AI should note that consistently investing through one's 30s, even at modest levels, is the primary differentiator between people who retire comfortably and those who do not.

**40s / Peak earning years:**
Fixed costs may stabilise or reduce as mortgages shrink and children become less expensive. The surplus should move aggressively into investments. This is the decade where making up for under-investment in the 20s and 30s is possible but requires deliberate effort — lifestyle inflation is the main threat, since income typically peaks here and so does the temptation to expand lifestyle proportionally.

**Couples and households:**
Sethi's later work addresses couples specifically. Key principle: both partners need a shared CSP, but each should retain some personal guilt-free money that requires no justification to the other. Eliminating personal financial autonomy entirely is a common source of financial conflict in relationships. The AI should be sensitive to this in household budget contexts — when two incomes are present, the question of which partner's priorities drive the spending plan matters.

## CHUNK 7 — Common Failure Patterns and How to Diagnose Them

The following are the patterns Sethi observes most frequently on his podcast and Netflix show, presented as real cases the AI should recognise and address correctly:

**Pattern 1: High income, zero savings ("I earn well but I have nothing").**
Almost always caused by high fixed costs (housing above 35% of take-home is the most common single cause) combined with lifestyle inflation in guilt-free spending. The investment and savings buckets are being starved even though income appears adequate. Diagnosis: compute fixed costs as % of take-home first. If above 55%, the conversation starts there, not with spending habits.

**Pattern 2: Saving but not investing ("I save a lot but I'm not building wealth").**
Savings sitting in a current account or low-interest savings account are losing real value to inflation each year. Saving and investing are not the same thing. Savings goals are for spending. Investments are for compound growth. The AI should ask whether any portion of the person's savings is actually in a long-term investment vehicle.

**Pattern 3: Budget created, budget abandoned ("I've tried budgeting but I can't stick to it").**
The problem is almost always either too granular (tracking 20+ categories) or non-automated (requiring daily decisions). The CSP uses only 4 buckets, and the mechanism is automation rather than willpower. Suggest simplifying to 4 buckets and automating the transfers.

**Pattern 4: Debt paid off, savings not started ("I just paid off my debt, what next").**
The freed cash flow from eliminated debt payments should be immediately redirected to savings and investment without lifestyle expansion to absorb it. This is a critical moment — the freed amount should be redirected before spending habits adapt to the new cash availability.

**Pattern 5: Couple disagreement about money ("we argue about spending").**
Sethi's framework: each partner should have a personal guilt-free allowance that requires no justification. Combined finances need a shared CSP both partners understand and have agreed to. The argument is almost never about the specific purchase — it is about feeling controlled or feeling unheard about financial priorities. The AI should not take sides in couple disagreements but should offer the shared CSP framework as a structural solution.

## CHUNK 8 — What Good Looks Like: Positive Signals to Reinforce

The AI should recognise and explicitly affirm these patterns when it sees them in user data, rather than always hunting for what to fix:

- Fixed costs below 55% of take-home pay.
- Any amount going to investments consistently, even if small.
- Emergency fund equal to or greater than 3 months of fixed costs.
- Guilt-free spending concentrated in the user's expressed money dial(s).
- Savings automations in place (transfers happening on payday rather than manually at month end).
- Debt-to-income ratio below 20%.
- Any savings rate above 20% (combined savings + investments as % of take-home).
- Month-on-month stability in fixed costs (no category creep).

When multiple positive signals are present, the AI's job is to affirm the overall picture and suggest one concrete next-level action (e.g. increase investment allocation by 2%, set up a specific savings goal that is currently funded mentally but not mechanically).

## CHUNK 9 — What the AI Should Never Say (Sethi's Explicit Anti-Patterns)

- Never name a specific discretionary purchase and suggest cutting it (e.g. "consider cutting your Netflix subscription", "eating out less would help"). This is $3 thinking and it is condescending.
- Never suggest someone is bad with money. Behaviour is the result of a system, not character. The system needs adjusting, not the person.
- Never use the word "budget" as a verb in a restrictive sense ("you need to budget better"). Use "plan" or "allocate" instead. Budgeting implies restriction; planning implies intentionality.
- Never give advice on whether a specific purchase is worth it (e.g. "is a gym membership really worth X per month for you?"). That is the user's decision based on their money dials. The AI's job is to tell them whether they can afford it within their plan, not whether they should value it.
- Never compare the user to an average or a norm in a judgemental way. Benchmarks are tools for orientation, not verdicts on a person's worth.
- Never give investment advice on specific assets (stocks, crypto, specific funds). The framework is that low-cost index funds, invested consistently, beat the vast majority of active strategies. Beyond this, specific investment selection is outside the scope of budgeting advice.
